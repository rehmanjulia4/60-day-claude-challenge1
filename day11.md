## Key Learnings

### 1. Resumes are positioning documents, not biographies

The same career history produces radically different ATS outcomes depending on which keywords lead and which roles are foregrounded. "Analytics leadership" and "clinical informatics leadership" describe the same experience. They surface in completely different executive recruiter searches.

### 2. Your experience is stronger than your resume

Executive search firms evaluate the gap between experience and presentation. A resume that undersells UCHealth, Presbyterian, and DOH Abu Dhabi is leaving real market value on the table. These are known systems with recognized scope. Name them and frame them correctly.

### 3. ATS gaps fall into two distinct categories

A **gap** is a keyword you have but have not named. This is a formatting and framing problem, solvable with optimization.

A **deficit** is a credential you genuinely do not hold. This requires earning the credential or targeting roles that do not require it. No prompt closes a credential deficit. Honest ATS optimization never invents.

### 4. Maintain three master resumes

One profile cannot serve all target verticals with equal effectiveness. Three master resumes — operations, analytics, clinical informatics and digital health — significantly improves interview conversion across executive search verticals.

### 5. Claude performs better than generic AI on executive positioning because context engineering works

A generic "optimize my resume" prompt returns keyword adjustments. A prompt built around executive search standards, specific recruiter firm evaluation criteria, and market positioning analysis returns a strategically repositioned document. Context is the differentiator.
