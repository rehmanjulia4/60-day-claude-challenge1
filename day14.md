## The Premise

Most job seekers evaluate whether they can *get* the job.

Executive leaders should evaluate whether they *should take* it.

E-HLOAF™ applies the same governance rigor used in organizational accreditation to executive career decision-making. It is a transferable framework capable of assessing any VP, CDO, CAIO, Executive Director, or C-Suite healthcare leadership opportunity across providers, payers, consulting firms, associations, regulators, and digital health organizations.

## Eight-Dimension Risk Scoring Model

| Dimension | Weight | What It Measures |
|-----------|--------|-----------------|
| Strategic Alignment | 15% | Role objectives vs. organizational strategy |
| Governance Clarity | 15% | Decision rights, reporting structure, board dynamics |
| Financial Stability | 15% | Compensation transparency, organizational fiscal health |
| Leadership Support | 15% | Executive sponsorship, C-suite alignment |
| Scope Realism | 15% | Accountability vs. resources vs. stated title |
| Culture Risk | 10% | Burnout signals, psychological safety, pace language |
| Compensation Transparency | 5% | Salary disclosure, incentive clarity |
| Growth Potential | 10% | Career trajectory, organizational investment in the function |

## Key Red Flags by Role

### Executive Director, Healthcare Association (Score: 28 - Low Risk)

**Red Flags:**
- Expected to simultaneously function as CEO, COO, fundraiser, lobbyist, and public spokesperson (Severity: 6/10)
- Board dynamics can significantly impact executive success trajectory (5/10)
- Political advocacy responsibilities create competing stakeholder priorities (4/10)

**Positive Signals:**
- Board-facing executive role with significant strategic autonomy
- Mission-driven organization with established community reputation
- Clear opportunity to shape organizational strategy and advocacy agenda

**Verdict:** Apply

---

### AVP Analytics, Enterprise Health System (Score: 42 - Moderate Risk)

**Red Flags:**
- Enterprise-wide expectations embedded in AVP title scope (8/10)
- Responsibility without commensurate decision-making authority (7/10)
- Multi-market support structures create competing priorities (6/10)
- Teams likely inheriting significant technical and governance debt (7/10)

**Positive Signals:**
- National scale with enterprise analytics investment trajectory
- Significant operational impact across multiple care settings
- Career progression pathway toward VP and CDO roles

**Verdict:** Apply with Caution

### VP Enterprise Transformation (Score: 67 - High Risk)

**Red Flags:**
- Transformation accountability with no budget ownership (10/10)
- Enterprise accountability significantly exceeds granted authority (10/10)

**Positive Signals:**
- Executive visibility and direct access to C-suite leadership
- Potential stepping stone toward COO or CAIO trajectory

**Verdict:** Apply with Extreme Caution

## Healthcare-Specific Red Flags (That Generic Frameworks Miss)

### Data and AI Leadership
- "Build AI strategy" with no governance framework or data quality program in place
- No executive data council or data governance charter referenced
- No responsible AI policy or clinical AI oversight structure
- Analytics expectations without data governance foundation
- No transformation budget allocated to the role

### Clinical and Population Health
- Value-based care accountability without data access or payer-provider alignment
- Population health goals without contracting or network authority
- Quality improvement goals without physician engagement structure
- Transformation goals without operational ownership at service-line level
- Equity mandates without community data infrastructure

## The Five Smart Interview Questions (Universal)

These apply to any executive healthcare leadership role:

1. Who owns the budget for this function, and what is the annual allocation?
2. Which decisions can I make independently versus requiring executive team consensus?
3. What circumstances led to this vacancy, and what is the typical tenure at this level?
4. How will success be defined and measured at the 6-month and 12-month marks?
5. Which C-suite leaders actively sponsor this initiative, and how is that sponsorship structured?

---

## Key Learnings

**01.** Executive roles fail more often because of governance problems than capability gaps.

**02.** Authority matters more than title. A VP without decision rights is a senior individual contributor.

**03.** A transformation role without executive sponsorship and budget is structurally designed for failure.

**04.** The best opportunities clearly define decision rights, success metrics, and resources upfront in the JD language itself.

**05.** Healthcare leaders should evaluate organizations with the same rigor applied to accreditation surveys. Ask the hard questions before accepting.

---

## The Core Question

Before accepting any executive leadership role:

> "Who owns the budget, who owns the decisions, and how will success be measured?"

That answer tells you more than the job description itself.
